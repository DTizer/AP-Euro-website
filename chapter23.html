Chapter 23: The Age of Nationalism

In this chapter, you will learn about the rising idea of nationalism after the revolutions of 1848, and also learn about the Republic of France and the unification of Italy and Germany. In addition, the chapter will cover reforms in Russia, Ottoman Empire, France, and Great Britain, and the chapter will finally address growing racism and anti-semitism.

Chapter Questions

Main Ideas

Russification: Believed by Alexander II, it is a form of cultural assimilation where non-Russian peoples must give up their culture and beliefs in favor of Russian Culture and Language.
Racism: Because of the idea of Social Darwinism, the idea of having a superior race became popular, and racism against ethnic minorities in European countries grew, such as against Poles, Czechs.
Anti-semitism: Hate in Jews heavily fostered during this period of growing anti semitism. Because of the stock market crash in 1873, many Europeans blamed Jews for having financial control and causing the crash. The Protocols of the Elders of Zion spread a false truth that the Jews held a secret meeting, planning to dominate the world.
Zionism: In response to anti semitism, Zionism began to emerge, which was a Jewish political movement that advocated for a Jewish state in Palestine and attempted to convince Christian Europeans to accept Jews.
Revisionism: an effort by socialists to update Marx’s doctrine to reflect the realities of time.

Important People/Groups

Louis Napoleon: Leader of the French Second republic, he promoted national unity and social progress. He had to share power with the National Assembly, which was very conservative. Hence, he dismissed the National Assembly in a coup, and Louis Napoleon declared himself emperor of France Napoleon III.
Napoleon III: He made various social and economic reforms, like creating new banks and building railroads. He also granted workers to form unions and the right to strike. He chose his own ministers, and kept the National Assembly, but the members were elected by universal male suffrage every 6 years. Soon problems with unification in Italy and war with Prussia caused Napoleon to grant France a constitution, which was heavily elected for, moving France in a democratic direction.
Mazzini: He called for a centralized democratic republic based on universal male suffrage and the will of the people.
Gioberti: he was a Catholic priest and called for federation of existing states under the presidency of a progressive pope. However, many Utalians looked to Sardinia-Piedmont for leadership.
Cavour: Leader of Sardinia, he worked towards a liberal constitutional state capable of leading northern Italy.
Garibaldi: He was very nationalist and republican, and he led a corps of volunteers, called the Red Shirts, to the southern Italian states like Palermo and Naples.
Red Shirts: Garibaldi’s army that used guerilla warfare to defeat the royal army in Italian southern states.
Victor Emmanuel II: Once North and South Italy had been united, Cavour and Garibaldi decided to let Victor Emmanuel II be king of Italy. He was neither radical nor completely democratic, and established a parliamentary monarchy in Italy.
William I: King of Prussia at his time, he wanted to reform the military and expand it, but the Prussian parliament rejected, so he appointed Bismarck as prime minister, who will become one of the greatest heroes of all time.
Bismarck: He was very pragmatic and flexible, keeping his options open to achieve his goal. First thing as prime minister, he defied the parliament by declaring William’s government would rule with parliamentary consent, and he went right on to collect taxes to fund and reorganize the army. In the following years, he went into a series of wars to earn German unification, which will be discussed later.
Alexander II: Tsar of Russia during this period, he established multiple reforms after the devastating Crimean War. His biggest change was that he abolished serfdom and created a zemstvo, which was a local assembly elected by the people. Despite serfdom being abolished, peasants had to own the land collectively, which limited the reforms.
Sergei Witte: Finance minister of Russia, he believed the industrial backwardness of Russia threatens its greatness. He established high protective tariffs and put the country on the gold standard.
Nicholas II: He took the place of his father, and it was during a time of Russian imperialism in Japan. THat led to a devastating Russo-Japanese War, which the Russians lost badly. This caused a revolution in Russia, which will be discussed later.
Muhammad Ali: He was a soldier politician that ruled Egypt and established reforms to industrialize Egypt.
Ibrahim: Ali’s son, he led Egyptian troops to occupy Ottoman governed province of Syria and Palestine, which led to conflicts with the Ottoman Empire.
Mahmud II: Mahmud II, sultan of the Ottoman Empire at this time, was rescued by the british and french, which took over finance in Egypt (Chapter 24). This was because the Europeans preferred a weak and dependent Ottoman empire as opposed to a strong and economically independent state like Egypt.
Abdulhamid II: He halted the Ottoman reform movements of the Tanzimat, but failed to halt foreign affairs. This led to a coup by the Young Turks, who forced him to implement reforms.
Young Turks: Officially known as Committee of Union and Progress, they staged a coup against Abdulhamid II and forced him to implement reforms, which paved the way for the birth of Turkey after the collapse of Ottoman Empire in WW1.
Reichstag: It was a popularly elected lower house compared to the Parliament, but it gave many political parties the chance to be part of government. Bismarck preferred to win the support of the Rechstag than Parliament.
German Social Democratic Party (SPD): Germany’s Marxist, working class political party that Bismarck wanted to stop.
Adolphe Thiers: Leader of the French National Assembly, he crushed the uprising in Paris and paved the way to France national unity of the Third Republic.
Gambetta: He was a moderate republican leader of France at this time.
Emile Zola: she wrote an article supporting Dreyfus in the Dreyfus affair, charging that the military judges had knowingly let the guilty party og, while Dreyfus remained imprisoned.
Alfred Dreyfus: He was a Jewish captain in the French army, and he was falsely convicted of treason, which led to the Dreyfus Affair.
House of Commons: the more democratic party of Great Britain.
House of Lords: made of nobles, they were mostly lazy and did not do much to reform the country.
Liberal Party: It came to power in 1906 and established the People’s Budget, looking to make Great Britain more liberal.
Lloyd George: He inspired the Liberal Party and soon became prime minister of Great Britain during WW1.
William Gladstone: He was the current prime minister of Great Britain, and he sought to pacify Ireland by introducing bills that allowed Ireland to be independent. However, they failed to pass, and the Ireland Revolution continued.
Francis Joseph: He was the King of Hungary, and he sought to unite and Germanize the multiple ethnic groups in Hungary.
Magyars: They were the biggest advocates for their own independence from Austria, which led to the division and creation of an Austro-Hungarian empire.
Gobineau and Chamberlain: They claimed that their ideas about racial difference were scientific, based on biological facts about bloodlines and heredity.
Karl Lueger & Christian Socialist Party: it was an anti semitic nationalist party that attacked and degraded Jews to win popular support.
Theodor Herzl: He was a leading Zionist that advocated for a jewish home country in Palestine.
Rise of Socialist Parties: These parties were heavily influenced by Marx’s ideas of socialism and revolution, and sought to create socialist states. However, during this time period, the socialist parties looked more towards gradual reform rather than immediate revolution.
Bernstein: he was a socialist and argued that Marx’s predictions had been proven false and socialists should reform their doctrines and tactics. He said that they should combine with other progressive forces to make gains for workers through legislation, unions, and economic development.
Jean Jaures: He was a socialist leader in France, and he repudiated revisionism in order to establish a unified socialist party, but he remained at heart a gradualist and optimistic secular humanist.

Important Treaties/Plans/Reforms

Zollverein: it was a German customs union that made it such that there were no tariffs among trade in the states in the union. Austria was not included in the Zollverein, which led Prussia to surpass them economically and grew a rivalry between Austria and Prussia.
Homestead Act of 1862: It was an act made in America after the civil war, which gave western land to settlers, reinforcing the concept of free labor taking its chances in a market economy.
October Manifesto: Granted the people of Russia civil rights and promised the creation of a parliament called the Duma.
Zemstvo: It was an elected local assembly established by Alexander II.
Duma: dominated by middle class liberals, it barely had power because it was elected by universal male suffrage and could debate and pass laws, but the tsar could veto them. Hence, Nicolas II eventually dismissed it.
Tanzimat: it was a series of reforms in the Ottoman empire designed to modernize the empire. The biggest reform is that it created equality before the law regardless of religious faith, administration of the army, and private ownership of land.
Kulturkampf: Bismarck wanted to attack the church, called the Kulturkampf which aimed at making the church subject to government control. It failed however, because Catholics generally voted for the Central Party, which blocked laws hostile to the church.
Great Reform Bills: gave more males in Great Britain voting rights.
People’s Budget: it was designed to increase spending on the social welfare services, but it was vetoed by the House of Lords.

Main Events

Unification of Italy: Cavour wanted to unify the northern part of Italy, but Austria was a challenge because it controlled several northern states. Hence, Cavour established an alliance with France, and they defeated Austria, gaining Lombardy. During the battle, Louis Napoleon was afraid of getting criticized by French Catholics for supporting the Italian pope, so he backed out. However, popular uprisings in central Italy desiring to merge with Sardinia grew strong, and the nationalistic ideas rose again. Cavour gained Napoleon II support by ceding Savoy and Nice to France. In the south, Garibaldi was leading his Red Shirts and taking control of the southern states, but Cavour interfered in Rome because he did not want Garibaldi to attack the pope. At Rome, Garibaldi decided to join Cavour and together they created a unified Italian state, establishing Victor Emmanuel II as king.
Austro-Prussian War: The conflict began with Schleswig-Holstein, two provinces that belonged to Denmark but were part of the German Confederation. The Danish king tried to bring these states together in a more centralized Denmark state, so Prussia and Austria came together to defeat Denmark and gain back the provinces. Bismarck saw that Prussia needed to control the northern part, which meant expelling Austria, so after neutralizing with France and Russia, he went to war with Austria, only lasting 7 weeks, with Prussia winning and forming the North German Confederation without Austria. Bismarck offered Austria generous peace terms, so Austria had to pay no reparations and lost no territory, only Venetia was ceded to Italy. Bismarck did this because he anticipated war against France in the future and did not want Austria to side with France in that war to take revenge on Prussia.
Franco-Prussian War: Now Bismark wanted the southern german states, and he decided waging a war against France would gain the southern states. With the Ems Dispatch, Bismarck angered France and forced France to declare war on Prussia. As soon as the war began, the southern German states supported Prussia, and they defeated France at the Battle of Sedan. After the victory, the southern states were willing to join, and at the Hall of Mirrors in Versailles, William I was declared emperor of the German Empire. Bismarck then imposed hard reparations on France. France had to pay indemnity of 5 billion francs and Alsace and Lorraine was given to Germany.
US Civil War: the North of the United States was much more industrialized, while the South continued to use slaves for agriculture. This led to a conflict between the balance between slave and non-slave states, Abraham Lincold became president, and 11 states left the Union and formed the COnfederate State of America, leading to Civil War.
Crimean War: It was a result of the breakdown of the balance of power established at the Congress of Vienna. It was between Great Powers for competition over the Middle East, and Russia’s desires to expand into the Ottoman Empire.
Russian Revolution 1905: Stemmed from military disaster in the Russo-Japanese War. Started on Bloody Sunday, troops opened fire on a peaceful gathering of workers at St. Pertersburg to present a petition to Nicholas II. Following that there were many peasant and worker uprisings, which were concluded with the October Manifesto.
Dreyfus Affair: Alfred Dreyfus was falsely accused of treason, but it split France apart. On one side, the army, anit-semites and most of the Catholic establishment were against Dreyfus, and on the other side, civil liberals and radical republicans supported for Dreyfus innocence. The resulting trial revived republican hatred against the Catholic Church.
Union Movement: Basically unions gained more rights and became more socialist to support economic and workers reforms.
