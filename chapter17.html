Chapter 17: The Expansion of Europe

In this chapter, you will learn about the growing population of European society, and how it led to the agricultural revolution and more ideas of free market and the rise of the working class. Finally the chapter will cover trade and race in Africa, Asia, and the Pacific.

Quizlet Flashcards

Main Ideas

Open Field system: The land was open and not closed off by any fences, but it was divided into strips, each owned by a different family of peasants. Three year rotations were implemented, which meant that they harvested for 3 years but then for one year it is left fallow for the nitrogen to be restored to the soil. This led to a slow process of farming and food shortages.
Enclosure movement: With the open field system, as scientists and farmers wanted to experiment with new crops, they could only do so with the permission of the landholders in the village to agree to the plan. This led to the development of enclosed fields, where farming could be more efficient.. Many peasant farmers were too poor and dependent on the system of open fields, so many noble landowners were the ones who put fences and enclosed their land, making the land individual shares and peasants soon lost their land.
Proletarianization: as noble landowners owned most of the land, they got profits by leasing the land to middle class farmers at high prices. These farmers relied on landless rural peasants for labor on the fields. These landless rural workers were known as proletariats, and led to the idea of proletarianization.
Putting out system: In this system, the merchant loaned, or “put out” raw materials to cottage workers, who processed the raw materials into product, and then gave them back to the merchant who sells them for a profit. The cottage workers were paid by piece, or how much they made. This system led to high production of domestic goods and textiles.
Industrious revolution: social economic changes during the early 1700s. Leisure time was reduced, the pace of work increased and it redirected the labor of women and children away from the production of goods for household consumption and toward wage work.
Economic liberalism: the idea that the pursuit of self interest in a competitive market would improve the living conditions of citizens.
Debt peonage: it was a system similar to serfdom, where a planter or rancher would keep workers in debt bondage on their land by providing them with food, shelter, and money. It was used by the Spanish on indigenous workers.
Trade rivalry in Pacific/Asia: in the 1500 Spain and Portugal dominated trade. In the 1600s, the Dutch rose to trade, and in the 1700s the British and France were the most dominant, while the Spanish, Portugal, and Dutch had fallen off. Dutch East India Company had controlled Portugal spice trade with India in 1602. However, the Dutch soon fell off as they failed to keep up with competition, and the English East India Company took over.

Important People/Groups

Jethro Tull: He invented the seed drill, which was more efficient in placing seeds in the perfect depth into the soil than by hand. He also experimented with using horses rather than oxen, and using selective breeding to improve livestock.
Arthur Young: He supported the enclosure movement, saying it was a means towards progress in agriculture.
Textile workers: They often lived in small houses crowded with multiple family members, because to spin the shuttle 4-5 spinners were needed. Sometimes they hired spinsters, who were unmarried or widowed women that were looking for work and got it by helping spin the loom.
Urban guilds: Guilds had many privileges, including exclusive rights to produce and sell certain goods, access to restricted markets in raw materials, and the rights to train apprentices, hire workers, and open shops. It was difficult to be part of a guild, and they restricted membership to local men who were Christians, had years of experience, paid membership fees, and completed a piece of work. Masters’ sons enjoyed automatic access.
Adam Smith: He argued for free markets and said that the government should provide a defense against foreign invasion, maintain civil order with courts and police protection, and sponsor public works that are unable to profit private investors.
Oliver Cromwell: He advocated for mercantilism in France, and initiated the Navigation Acts.
Creoles: It referred to people with Spanish ancestry born in the Americas. They were often wealthy and acted like European landowners, building colonial cities, purchasing goods, and sending their children to education.

Important Acts/Treaties

Navigation Acts: it required goods imported from England to be carried on English ships with British crew. It gave British shipowners and merchants monopolies over British colonial trade, and it was a form of economic warfare against the Dutch, who were ahead of the British during the 1650s.
Peace of Utrecht: Resolution to the war of Spanish Succession, Louis XIV of France to cede his North American holdings in Newfoundland, Nova Scotia, and the Hudson Bay territory to Britain. Spain was compelled to give Britain control of its West African slave trade, called the asiento, and to let Britain send one ship of merchandise into the Spanish colonies annually.
Treaty of Paris: Resolution to the Seven Years War, Britain gained victory on all colonial fronts and Prussia had won against the Austrians.

Main Events

War of spanish Succession: Louis XIV placed his grandson on the Spanish throne, which threatened the balance of power and also France and Spain could combine powers to destroy British colonies. After 12 years of fighting, Louis XIV lost, and it was resolved with the Peace of Utrecht.
War of Austrian Succession: It began when Frederick the Great of Prussia seized Silesia from Austria’s Maria Theresa. France sided with Austria, and Great Britain sided with Prussia which spread into Anglo French conflicts in the colonies, once again demonstrating the heavy competition between France and England for colonial control. In the end, the British won on all colonial fronts, once again.
