Chapter 26: Age of Anxiety

This chapter focuses on the aspects of western life after the first world war, and the events and industrial advancements that led to the second world war. The chapter will go through new modern philosophical ideas, new physics and science theories, architecture, artistic, and musical advancements. Throughout the chapter, many important people are mentioned, so the content of this page will list all the people and their ideas for simpler understanding.

Main Ideas

Existentialism: Reason and science are incapable of providing insight into the human situation. God, reason and progress are no such things, humans rather live in a world alone and isolated. A person is a sum of his or her actions.
Logical Positivism: Argued that what we know about human life must be based on rational facts and direct observation. Concluded theories and religion are useless because they cannot be proven using logic.
Revival of Christianity: These thinkers believed it was impossible to prove that God existed, but still emphasized that people should still believe in God.
Freudian Psychology: The Id, Ego, and Superego. The id is the sexual desires of the unconscious, the superego is the internalized voice of parental or social control, which balances the id. The ego was the rational self that worked to balance the demands of the id and the superego.
Functionalism: Buildings should serve a purpose for which they were made, like industrial products.
Impressionism: A form of art that portrayed the real parts of life, rather than traditional themes like battles, religion, or wealthy elites.
Expressionism: Built on the impressionist motifs but added a psychological element to invoke emotion in their art
Cubism: Approach to art that concentrated on complex geometric shapes overlapping each other to form an abstract, nonrepresentational art.
Dadaism: It was a radical form of art that was considered as “anti-art”. Dadaists believed life had no meaning, so art should have no meaning either.
Surrealism: Another form of art that painted worlds of wild dreams and uncomfortable symbols, emphasizing the Freudian psychology of the unconscious. It is very important to note that dadaism and Surrealism was a radical form of art.
Stream-of-consciousness technique: A form of writing technique that relied on internal monologues to explore the human psyche.
Modern Music: Modern music abandoned the traditional use of harmonies and musical notes were no longer united and organized by a key. The musical notes were arranged in a mathematical pattern.
Modern Girl: The idea of an independent female that could vote and had a job, and spent her money on cigarettes and drinks, makeup, and used sex appeal to charm young men.

Important People/Groups

Nietzsche: Believed reason, progress, and respectability were outworn social norms that limited self realization and excellence. He also rejected religion and painted a picture of the world, that it was entering a state of nihilism, which means human life has no meaning at all.
Bergson: Argues immediate experience and intuition were as important as rational scientific thinking. According to him, religious ideas are more accessible to human than science and math
Wittgenstein: Argued philosophy is the only logical clarification of thoughts. Believed god and liberal ideas were senseless because they could not be proven with science.
Kierkegard: Believed it was impossible to prove the existence of God, but argued people must take a “leap of faith” and accept a majestic God.
Sartre: His famous saying, “existence precedes essence” means that only after they are born do people struggle to define their essence, all on their own. He concluded most people escape radical freedom by structuring their lives around social norms. Instead, to live an authentic life, Sartre argued individuals must choose their own actions and become fully responsible for their own behavior
Barth: Argued that human beings were imperfect, sinful creatures whose reasons were absolutely flawed. Said religious truth is made known only through God’s grace, not reason. He concluded people have to accept God’s word and religion, not reason or logic.
Curie, Planck, Einstein, Rutherford: Revolutionary scientists that developed new theories and discoveries. Their ideas defied Newton’s dependable laws, and instead made the world seem as unstable and uncertain.
Freud: Concluded human behavior is irrational, governed by the unconscious, which is unknowable to the conscious mind, leaving people unaware of the meaning of their actions.
Gropius: Merged applied arts schools into a Bauhaus. It brought together many architects, designers, and innovators, who all worked together to study art and its functions.
Van Gogh: Very famous expressionist, who used radical strokes of art to reflect the self and reveal deeper feelings in the canvas.
Picasso: Famous artist who established cubism.
Joyce: Most famous author that experimented with stream of consciousness technique
Kafka: Had a pessimistic view of society and portrayed individuals crushed bu hostile forces in his novels.
Schonberg: well known composer for abandoning harmonies in musical pieces and for arranging the 12 notes in a mathematical pattern
Keynes: Believed harshed reparations on Germany would encourage revolution and increase hardship on other European countries. His idea was that European countries worked together as a whole organization to support each other, and if one country’s economy fails, all the rest will.
Poincare: French Prime minister who strictly wanted Germany to pay reparations for causing World War 1
Stresseman: After France occupied Ruhr, Stresseman took over the government and called off passive resistance, forming a compromise with France on how to pay the reparations.
Communist Party: Hated the Social Democrats Party
Social Democrats: Hated the Communist Party
Liberal Party: Taken over by the Labour Party. It meant a decline in liberal ideals of competitive capitalism, limited government control, and individual responsibility.
Labour Party: Replaced the Liberal Party.
Workers Progress Administration (WPA): It was an organization that hired lots of workers to construct public buildings, bridges, and highways.

Important Books/Treaties/Plans

Untimely Meditations (Nietzsche): Argued that since classical Athens, the West has overemphasized rationality and stifled the authentic passions and animal instincts that drive human activity and creativity.
On Genealogy of Morals (Nietzsche): Claimed that Christianity embodied a “slave morality” that glorified weakness, envy, and mediocrity.
Civilization and Its Discontents (Freud): Argued civilization is only possible when individuals renounced their irrational instincts in order to live peaceably in groups
Ulysses (Joyce): abandon conventional plot, breaking rules of grammar, and combine memories together in a confusing way, it is meant to reflect the complexity of modern life.
The Trial and The Castle (Kafka): Stories about helpless individuals crushed by inexplicably hostile forces.
The Economic Consequences of the Peace (Keynes): Denounced the treaty of Versailles and demanded a complete revision of the treaty to save Germany and Europe.
Dawes Plan: Germany reparations were reduced and they would receive loans from the United States to pay off reparations to France and Britain, who then can repay large debts they have to the US.
Kellogg-Briand Pact: It “renounced war as an instrument of international policy” and settled international disputes peacefully. Made no provisions for action in case war actually occurred, so it could not prevent WW2
US New Deal: In response to the Great Depression, Roosevelt wanted to reform capitalism and preserve it. Advocated for government control of the economy and established government supported social programs to provide jobs.
National Labor Relations Act: Intended to reduce competition among industries by setting minimum wage and prices. Broke off the American tradition of free competition, and was eventually abandoned in 1955.
Popular Front: Communists, Socialists, and Radicals formed a political alliance against the Fascists.

Main Events

Germany Reparations after WW1: Germany declared they could not pay anymore after one year, so France got mad and Poincare sent France troops to occupy Ruhr. The people of Ruhr stopped work to offer passive resistance, but that caused a decline in Germany’s economy. Stresseman took control of the government and called off the resistance, and formed a compromise with France and now everyone is happy.
The Great Depression: t began in the US when there were imbalances between business investment and stock market speculation. A lot of people had been borrowing money, so when the market crashed, everyone withdrew money from the banks, causing them to go bankrupt. European countries then struggled to borrow money so their economies also fell.
Scandinavian Response to Great Depression: Implemented more social reforms like old age pensions, retirement pensions, etc.
