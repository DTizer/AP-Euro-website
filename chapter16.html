Chapter 16: Toward a New Worldview

In this chapter you will learn about the Scientific Revolution, and the most impactful period of ideas, the Enlightenment, and all its people and beliefs, and how it affected absolutist rule in the 18th century.

Quizlet Flashcards

Main Ideas:

Natural Philosophy: focused on fundamental questions about the universe and how it worked and its purpose.
Copernican Hypothesis: he hypothesized the sun was the center of the universe, not the earth. It implied that the stars are at rest, which destroyed the ideas of crystal spheres around earth, and it implied that the universe was really large, and he used math to prove his hypothesis, which challenged traditional hierarchy of disciplines.
Experimental method: Galileo’s idea that instead of speculating about what might or should happen, people should conduct experiments to find out what actually did happen.
Law of inertia: founded by Galileo, an object stays in motion forever unless stopped by an external force.
Law of universal gravitation: founded by Newton, it said that every body in the universe attracts every other body in the universe, in a precise mathematical relationship.
Empiricism: decisions should not be made based on speculation, but rather careful observation and calculation to prove and make claims.
Cartesian Dualism: Descartes' idea that everyone had a physical and spiritual part of themselves which is because God had given man reason and purpose and rational speculation could provide truth of creation.
Public Sphere: is a space where members come together as individuals and discuss issues relevant to society, politics, and economics of the day.
Enlightened Absolutism: The idea that absolutist leaders should reform their government according to Enlightenment ideals.
Cameralism: it was a German idea that monarchy was the best form of government, and the society should be of service to the state and in turn the state should use resources and authority to improve society.

Important People/Groups

Aristotle & Ptolemy: They both believed that the Earth was the center of the universe. Potelmy believed the planets moved in small circles each that moved along a larger circle. Aristotle believed the celestial spheres around earth contained a “quintessence”, while Earth had four elements. Both of their ideas fit with the Christian Doctrine, so churches relied on their ideas and constantly believed in them.
Copernicus: wrote “On the revolutions of the heavenly spheres” he challenged Aristotle and Ptolemy and claimed that the sun was the center of the universe, not the earth.
Brahe: He created many sophisticated observatories, and he created the Rudolphine Tables, tables of planetary motions. He believed all planets besides the earth moved around the sun.
Kepler: He demonstrated that orbits of planets around the sun are elliptical, not circular, and they do not move at the same speed and it depends on how close they are to the sun.
Galileo: He used the experimental method, founded the law of inertia, and discovered the 4 moons of Jupiter. He was a Catholic and kept his ideas and views to himself not wanting to challenge the church, but he eventually wrote a book that made fun of Aristotle and Potelmy’s ideas and supported Copernicus’s.
Newton: His use of mathematics and science to make new laws that would still be used today showed that the new methods of empiricism were rising and changing the way the Europeans see the world.
Bacon: He was a heavy believer and supporter of empiricism.
Descartes: he discovered analytical geometry. He also developed the idea that matter was made up of corpuscles that collided endlessly. It implied that a vacuum was impossible. His biggest belief was that one must doubt everything at first, and then use deductive reasoning to prove and confirm scientific laws. He also created the idea of Cartesian Dualism.
Pierre Bayle: he was a French Hugenot, and founded the idea of skepticism, which concluded that nothing can ever be known beyond all doubt
Baruch Spinoza: He believed the mind and body are the same, and that God and Nature are the same thing.
Montesquieu: he promoted liberty in government and wanted to prevent Tyranny. He criticized the rule of Louis XIV. he argued for separated shared powers between states and classes, admiring the English balance of power. He also believed the 13 high courts of France, the parlement were defenders of liberty against despotism.
Voltaire: he did not believe human beings were worthy to govern themselves, and people could only hope for a good monarch. He praised Louis XIV and admired Frederick the Great of Prussia. He believed the only equality was the laws that protected freedom of feeble citizens from the strong. He was a deist, like many other philosophers at this time, believing God created the universe but then ceased to intervene in human affairs.
Diderot & d’Alembert: both of them wrote the first Encyclopedia, many things were questioned and challenged, providing knowledge to the aspects of life during that time.
Rousseau: He supported individual freedom but attacked rationalism and civilization for destroying the individual. He wanted the basic goodness of the individual to be protected from refinements of civilization. His ideas greatly influenced the Romantic movement. He called for rigid division of gender. In his The Social Contract, he said that the general will is sacred and reflected the common interests of all the people, but it was not always the will of the majority, which contributed to early American and French revolutions ideas.
David Hume: argued the human mind is nothing but a bundle of impressions. He criticized human reasoning and undermined the Enlightenment ideas of power of reason.
Adam Smith: argued that commercial life of the eighteenth century produced civic virtue through the values of competition, fair play, and individual autonomy. In his Wealth of Nations, he attacked laws and regulations that prevented commerce from reaching full capacity.
Kant: argued that if individuals allowed freedom to exercise reason in print, it would lead to enlightenment. He also indicted individuals must obey all laws and must be punished for rude criticism. With his ideas, he tried to reconcile absolute monarchical authority and religious faith with a critical public sphere.
Frederick the Great of Prussia: He allowed his subjects to believe as they wished in religious and political matters. He promoted advancement of knowledge, bureaucracy laws simplified, torture was abolished, and judges decided cases quickly. After the Seven Years' War he reconstructed agriculture and industry. His rule and reforms followed ideas of Enlightened Absolutism, and he justified monarchical rule in practical terms, and not divine rights of kings.
Catherine the Great of Russia: She had 3 goals to her rule: Continue Peter the Great's effort to westernize russia. She brought in many western art, music and literature, and also supported the Western philosophes, gaining their support. Her second goal was for domestic reform, but it failed and was challenged by Pugachev, a Cossack soldier. Her third goal was territorial expansion, which she achieved with the Partition of Poland. It was when Russia defeated the Ottomans, leading to an unbalance of power, and then Austria, Russia, and PRussia decided to split Poland among themselves. The partitions of Poland took place in 1772, 1793, and 1795.
Maria Theresa: Ruler of the Austrian Habsburgs at this time, she also made many reforms that followed Enlightenment ideals. She initiated church reform, renovated the central bureaucracy, and improved the agricultural population. She was then replaced with Joseph II, who continued her reforms and perfect the state bureaucracy, like many other absolutists in the late 18th century.
