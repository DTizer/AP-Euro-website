Chapter 28: Cold War Conflict and Consensus

This chapter will talk about the aftermath of World War II, and how increased tensions and resolutions to the war with treaties led to a division between the east and west, and ultimately led to the Cold War.

Main Ideas:

Socialist Realism: Artists in communist states were forced to conform to this idea, which idealized working classes and the Soviet Union.
de-Stalinization: liberation from communist influences and rule in the East bloc.
Decolonization: European countries retreat from imperial control
Nonalignment: de colonized countries chose to remain neutral in the Cold War and played for both sides what they could get.
Neocolonialism: system designed to continue European economic domination in colonies and undermine the promise of political independence, even despite being de-colonized
Postcolonial migration: movement of people from former colonies to Europe.

Important People/Groups

Displaced persons: concentration camp survivors, released prisoners of war, thousands of orphaned children. They could not go home because of political instability or destroyed homes and neighborhoods during the war.
Big Three: Stalin, Roosevelt, and Churchill.
Council for Mutual Economic Assistance: an economic organization to rebuild the East bloc independently from the West.
NATO: anti-Soviet military alliance of Western governments.
Christian Democrats: new political party that offered center right recovery of society. They opposed competing left wing parties and pushed for social and economic reform. They rejected authoritarianism and encourage democracy and liberalism
Organization for European Economic Cooperation & the Council of Europe: both promoted commerce and cooperation among European countries.
Jean Monnet & Robert Shuman: French statesmen that called for an international organization to control Europe steel and coal production.
European Coal and Steel Community: it was a transitional market for steel and coal without tariffs or quotas. Consisted of Belgium, Netherlands, Italy, Luxembourg, France, and Great Britain.
Common Market: encouraged trade between European states, promoted global exports, and helped build shared resources for modernization of national industries.
Charles de Gaulle: He saw the US as a threat to French independence, so he left NATO, developed France’s own nuclear weapons, and vetoed majority rule within the Common Market.
Josip Broz Tito: Resistance leader and Communist chief of Yugoslavia, managed to proclaim independence because there was no Soviet Union Army to stop him.
Nikita Kuruschev: he became the new Soviet premier in 1955. He criticized Stalin’s rule and made reforms in Russia to de-stalinize. Shifted industry from heavy industry to consumer goods and relaxed Stalinist workplace controls. He boasted about the East consumer growth and compared it with the West, leading to the Kitchen Debate between him and Nixon.
Boris Pasternak: wrote Doctor Zhivago, tells the story of a poet who rejected violence and brutality of the October Revolution and criticized communism.
Aleksandar Solzhenitsyn: wrote One Day in the Life of Ivan Denisovich, portrays a grim life in Stalinist concentration camp, a life which he had condemned.
Imre Nagy: he was a liberal Communist reformer who proposed to democratize Hungary. He demanded open, multiparty elections. He announced that Hungary would leave the Warsaw Pact and asked the UN to protect Hungary neutrality, SU got alarmed and marched troops into Hungary to crush the revolution. The UN did not come to Hungary aid because it was busy with the Suez Crisis.
Leonid Brehnez: Replaced Khrushchev and led to a period of re-stalinization and economic stagnation. He proceeded cautiously to avoid conflict with the United States.
Ho Chi Mihn: Led guerilla forces to defeat France when France was trying to regain control of Vietnam. He was aided by the USSR and China. Led to a split: COmmunist North and Pro western South.
Mohandas Gandhi: He built a massive nonviolent movement of noncooperation with the British. He created a new liberal constitution from British imperialism and they withdrew peacefully. However, conflict between Hindus and Muslims still lasted. Led to Muslim territories being divided from India and forming Pakistan.
Jiang Jieshi: he led the authoritarian Guomindang party, also known as the Kuomintang National People’s Party.
Mao Zedong: Led the Chinese Communists, and had popular support. He was aided by the SU and Jiang was aided by the Americans, but the Communist gained support of peasants and ultimately forced the Guomindang to withdraw to Taiwan. The new government was led by Mao Zedong and became a communist, Stalin like government.
Gamal Abdel Nasser: Led a national revolution in Egypt, which managed to drive out the pro-WEstern King and Nasser became president of the Egyptian Republic, following the policy of nonalignment.
Patrice Lumumba: He led the Congo revolution against Belgian leaders. Belgians eventually gave in and promised Congo will be independent 6 months later, and Lumumba was chosen Prime Minister in democratic elections. When the Belgians left the government was very unprepared and chaos broke out. The US and western EU worry about the problem because they have financial investments in the COngo and are worried it might fall into SU hands, the CIA led a coup against Lumumba and a US backed dictatorship was set up under Joseph Mobutu. He became the wealthiest man in the world I guess.
National Liberation Front: Established by Algerians to revolt against French colonial rule. French living in Algeria were called Pied-Noirs, and worried about the revolution they asked the French for help, which led to a war of 400k French troops to crush the revolt. Charles de Gaulle was reinstated to maintain Algeria French, but in the end he peacefully declared Algerian independence.

Important Treaties/Plans

Nuremberg Trials: Allied power tried high ranking Nazi Officers and charged them with war crimes and crimes against humanity.
Truman Doctrine: it was a policy that promised that the US will use diplomatic, economic, and military means to prevent the spread of Communism. This was used to help during the Greek Civil War and counter SU expansion into Turkey.
Marshall Plan: George C. Marshall offered Europe economic aid to help it rebuild. It was only given to 15 Western Europe countries, because the East was not allowed to by Stalin.
Warsaw Pact: it was a military alliance between the USSR and all of its Communist satellites. Basically an alliance of all the Eastern Bloc countries.
Bretton Woods Agreement: It linked Western currencies to the US dollar and established the international Monetary fund and the World Bank to facilitate free market and trade.
Treaty of Rome: it gradually reduced tariffs to create a single market and created free movement of capital and labor and economic policies and institutions.
Guest worker programs: programs designed to recruit much needed labor for the booming economy.
