Chapter 19: Revolutions in Politics

In this chapter, you will explore the American Revolution and the French Revolution’s causes, events, and effects. Then you will learn about the rule of Napoleon after the French REvolution, the downfall of his rule, and ultimately the Haitian Revolution, the only successful slave revolt of all time.

Quizlet Flashcards

Important People/Groups

Bourgeoisie: Wealthy noble families that gain their wealth from industrial growth like manufacturing and trade. They were very high above the other classes, like the middle class and the peasants.
Liberals: They believed people had sovereignty, which meant the people had the authority to make laws limiting individual’s freedom. They wanted a new system of government where decisions would be made by legislators that are chosen by the people, limiting the power of monarchy.
Locke: argued if government overstep its function to protect the rights of the people, including life, liberty, and property, it becomes a tyranny
Montesquieu: believed powerful intermediary groups like judicial nobility offered the best defense of liberty against despotism.
Maria Theresa: It was a war that was caused by conflicts unresolved in the war of the Austrian Succession. Maria Theresa of Austria allied with France against the British and the Prussians, wanting to take back Silesia and to crush Prussia to re-establish Habsburg rule. Maria Theresa almost succeeded but failed, leaving Prussian territory intact.
Thomas Paine: he mocked the idea of a small island ruling a continent, and called for freedom and republican government. Wrote the famous “Common Sense”
Loyalists: They maintained loyalty to the British government controlling the United States.
Lafayette: Most trusted general of George Washington
Constitutional Convention: After gaining independence from Great Britain, they formed a new constitution that made senators and congressmen lawmaking delegates of voters, and the president would be an elected official. They also used Montesquieu’s framework of checks and balances, forming the executive, legislative, and judicial branches.
Louis XV: French king from 1715-1774. His rule was heavily influenced by Madame de Pompadour, which made the monarchical rule of Louis XV seem unsacred and corrupted. He made many bad decisions.
Louis XVI: Eventually Louis XV died, and Louis XVI took over, wishing to improve the economic conditions of France.
Estate General: It was made up of 3 estates. The first estate was made up of the clergy, the 2nd made of the nobility, and the 3rd made of everyone else. Voting rights in the Estates General was heavily unfair against the 3rd estate, because each estate was given one vote, and the 1st and 2nd estate would always outvote the third.
Emmanuel Joseph Sieyès: Despite being part of the first estate, he wrote that the nobility were a tiny over privileged minority and the third estate had more power and strength to rule France.
National Assembly: Angry about the voting system of the estates general, the 3rd estate refused to meet and broke off to form the National Assembly. They moved to a indoor tennis court to take the famous Tennis Court Oath, which they swore never to disband until they had written a new constitution.
Olympe de Gouges: She protested the evils of slavery and the injustices done to women. She wrote the Declaration of the Rights of Woman, where she claimed women should be equal to man in rights
Edmund Burke: he was a conservative leader in Britain and predicted that reform like the ones being imposed by the National Assembly in France would cause chaos.
Mary Wollenstonecraft: She also demanded equal rights for women and advocated for their education, believing women will be better mothers, wives, and citizens.
Legislative Assembly: The new representative body in France that replaced the National Assembly, it consisted of radical leaders called the Jacobins. After the Declaration of Pillnitz, the Legislative Assembly went to war with Prussia and Austria, which ended really badly.
National Convention: Replaced the Legislative Assembly after the fall of Louis XIV monarchy. They were once again dominated by the Jacobins, but it was split into the Girondists and the Mountain. When the National COnvention convicted Louis XVI of treason, the Girondists did not wish to execute him, but the Mountains had majority of the votes and Louis XVI was executed. Both still wanted the war against tyranny, and continued to invade Prussia and Austria states.
Sans-culottes: radical peasants known to have led lots of uprisings during the revolution, and they were called that because they wore trousers instead of traditional knee breeches. Together with the Mountain, they invaded the National Convention and arrested all the Girondists, giving the Mountain full power.
Committee of Public Safety: Formed by the National Convention, it was made to deal with threats inside and outside France. It was led by Robespierre, and acted as more of a dictatorial power to protect the revolution.
Robespierre: Leader of the Committee of Public Safety, he controlled the people by enforcing fixed prices, and forcing them to work the war effort, leading to nationalized workshops and gain in raw materials, unintentionally producing a form of socialism.
The Directory: After the overthrow of Robespierre, the National Convention formed the Directory, which gave power to a 5 man body. It ended up being very corrupt which led to a coup.
Napoleon Bonaparte: He led a coup against the directory, and became first consul of the First French Republic. He gained his popularity from leading armies in Italy where he gained large victories.
Alexander I: King of Russia during this time, he repudiated Napoleon’s war of prohibitions against British goods, leading to France invading Russia.
Grand Empire: Napoleon vastly conquered European states to the east, leading to an expansion of France just as he desired. Along the way he was challenge by the Coalitions of Europe, a number of Alliances between the Eastern and Central states to take down Napoleon
Louis XVIII: After the fall of Napoleon, the Bourbon dynasty was restored to power in France under his name, and he ruled with lenient peace terms from the Coalitions, accepting the Constitutional Charter, making his rule a constitutional monarchy.
Vincent Oge: He led an uprising of his army to free slaves in Haiti, but he was crushed and executed. However, his cause was successful as the National Assembly granted political rights to free people of color born to two free parents that had sufficient property.
Toussaint L’Ouverture: during the revolutionary wars of France, Britain invaded the French colony of Spain. L’Ouverture was at first a freed slave who joined the revolt against France control of the colony and slaves. He joined the Spanish army and became a general. However, when France said that they would emancipate anyone who helped them fight against the invading British, L’Ouverture switched sides and helped the French and eventually gained control of the colony, defeating the British.
Andre Rigaurd: he belonged to the free colored elite in Haiti, and set up his own government in the south. He despised the rule of L’Ouverture, and accused them of adopting the racism of the white settlers, leading to a civil war.
Jean Jacques Dessalines: L'Ouverture lieutenant in the civil war.

Important Treaties/Plans

Stamp Act: passed by Parliament onto the United States, it levied taxes on a long list of commercial and legal documents, diplomas, newspapers, almanacs, and playing cards. A stamp glued to each article indicated that the tax had been paid.
Tea Act: it permitted East India company to tea from China directly to agents in colonies, rather than through the London middlemen. Hence the company gained a monopoly on the tea trade, and colonial merchants were excluded.
Declaration of Independence: it universalized the traditional rights of the English people and made them the rights of all mankind. It also listed the tyrannical acts committed by George III, ruler of Great Britain during this time.
Bill of Rights: After the independence of the United States, the convention passed the Bill of rights that consisted of a list of rights to the American citizen.
Declaration of Rights of Man and of the Citizen: Passed by the National Assembly, it gave the citizens of France traditional rights.
Civil Constitution of the Clergy: passed by the national assembly, it established a national church with priests chosen by voters. It then forced the clergy to take an oath swearing loyalty to the new government. The pope condemned these measures and only half the priests of France took the oath.
Declaration of Pillnitz: declared by the monarchs of Austria and Prussia, it professed their willingness to intervene in France to restore Louis XVI rule if necessary.
Napoleonic Code: Issued by Napoleon, it reasserted equality of all male citizens before the law and security of wealth and private property.
Concordat of 1801: Pope obtained right for French Catholics to practice religion freely, but Napoleon’s government had the power to nominate bishops and they also paid the clergy.
Continental System: it blocked ships from Britain from docking at any French controlled ports. It was a war act by Napoleon to starve the British economy.
Treaty of Chaumont: Austria and Prussia joined Great Britain and Russia to take down the French Empire.
Constitutional Charter: it was a constitution that maintained France’s revolutionary changes and guaranteed civil liberties, a constitution that Loui XVI ruled with and later Louis Napoleon.

Main Events

Storming of Bastille: as grain harvest declined and bread prices increased, the people of France grew angry and decided to revolt. Moreover, Louis XVI kicked his finance minister, the last hope for the people to maintain a stable economy. When they heard that the king had sent troops to Paris, the people stormed the Bastille, to gain weapons for the city’s defense. This led to Louis XVI announcing reinstatement of the finance minister and withdrawing troops from Paris.
Great Fear: As revolution spread, peasants also gained revolutionary ideals and ransacked their manor houses and burning feudal records. Peasants also reoccupied land and forests.
Women’s march to Versailles: Women also gained revolutionary ideals and hated the shortage and increasing prices of bread, so they marched to Versialles demanding the royal family move to Paris to witness the poor living conditions. They also hated and criticized Marie Antoinette, the corrupted wife of Louis XVI that was oblivious to the living conditions in Paris.
Reign of Terror: Initiated by Robespierre, it was a period of mass execution of citizens and leaders who were suspected of betraying the revolution.
Thermidorian Reaction: After the French won against the First Coalition, they wanted to relax economic controls of the Committee of Public Safety, but Robespierrre’s reign of terror continued. He had killed many of his critics, so he was turning to his previous collaborators to execute. Afraid that they might be next, members of the Convention raided Robespierre’s speech and executed him. This led to reassertion of authority, and back to the radical governments.
Napoleonic wars: A series of wars against the Coalitions during Napoleon’s expansion of France into Eastern Europe.
Haitian Revolution: Seeing the conflict, Napoleon sent his french general and army to arrest L’Ouverture. This left Dessalines to continue to fight for Haitian Independence. He led a defeat over French forces, and he declared the state independent, then ratified the Haitian Constitution in 1805.
