Chapter 27: Dictatorships and the Second World War

In this chapter, we will explore the rising dictatorships of Stalin, Mussolini, and Hitler, the ideas of Communism and Facism, and how their dictatorships led to World War 2, including diplomacy and relations between the countries of Europe that ultimately caused World War 2. The chapter then explains the events of World War 2, and the ultimate victory of the Allies.

Main Ideas

Conservative Authoritarianism: Conservative Authoritarianism is an antidemocratic form of government. This form of government relied on bureaucracies and efforts to control society. They sought to suppress revolutionary enemies and ideologies that would change the authoritarian form of government. They often jailed or exiled political opponents, but did not have ability or desire to control their subjects. Examples are Catherine the Great in Russia and Metternich.
Totalitarianism: This form of government was rising during the period before World War 2, and were considered radical dictatorships. Totalitarian states had their own ideas and desires for what the state should look like as a whole. One-party totalitarian states such as Fascist and Communist Dictatorships used violent political repression and propaganda to gain power. They also dominated social, economic, and intellectual aspects of people’s lives.
Communism: The idea of Communism is inspired by Karl Marx's ideas of equality. Communists wanted a utopia ruled by the working class and there would be no more economic exploitation of capitalism. Society would be based on radical social equality and radical actions are needed to achieve a communist state.
Facism: Facism was embraced by Hitler and Mussolini, who claim they were building a state on the national level. Fascists supported war and the military, and also intervened in economics. They believed the nation was above the people, and the leader of the nation was a figure of the people’s will. Unlike communism, facism did not level class differences, but rather desired a community bound together by nationalism. Fascists also wanted to build a community where everyone was equal in race.
Eugenics: Embraced by Fascists, eugenics is a scientific belief that selective human breeding can improve the characteristics of a national population.
National Socialism: Also known as Nazism, national socialism was a combination of nationalism and racism. National Socialism was the idea that the state must be “purified”, and in the case of Nazi Germany, was to maintain German domination and rid the state of other ethnic classes, like Jews. It is important to note national socialism did not have any socialist ideals.

Important People/Groups

Lenin: Leader of the Bolsheviks during the Russian Revolution and the Russian Civil War, eventually came out victorious but Russia was in shambles. Lenin established economic and social reforms, but he died in 1924 without a chosen successor.
Trotsky: Leader of the Red Army during the Russian Civil War, he has a lot of leadership qualities and would seem like the better option against Stalin. He believed socialism in the Soviet Union could succeed only if socialist revolution swept throughout Europe.
Stalin: Stalin’s actual name is Joseph Dzhugashvili. He was a good organizer but a poor speaker and writer, and had no experience outside or Russia. He was a secretary of the party’s General Committee, so he used his position to win friends and allies with jobs and promises. Stalin won the election because he believed in socialism in one country, which was more appealing to party members, and he also wanted to remove the NEP, which many people supported because it was capitalist.
Kulaks: They were a class of well-off peasants who had benefited from the NEP. Stalin called for the liquidization and seizure of their land during his collectivization plan. Many Kulaks were starved to death or deported to forced labor camps.
Mussolini: Was a Socialist Party Leader and radical newspaper editor. He got kicked from the party and formed his own group of Fascists, and he became an enemy of socialism. He formed his own private militia called the Black Shirts, which helped Musollini push Socialists out of Italy. Once he gained power to rule Italy, he limited the rights of citizens and put the state under full Fascist control and promoted traditional gender roles.
Hitler: Began his political journey by joining the German Workers party. He took control of the party and renamed it National Socialist German Workers Party, or Nazis. He used a lot of propaganda and speeches to gain support, and eventually led an uprising in Munich called the Beer Hall Putsch, which failed. He believed in a purified german state with one ultimate leader, and also believed that Germans needed living space, so he desired to conquer European lands and spread Germany’s territory.
SA: Nazi stormtroopers, basically the Nazi’s private army. Consisted of 3 million people in brown shirts, and they fought communists and beat up Jews before Nazi party came to power. Htiler wanted the support of traditional military after he gained power, so he needed to get rid of the SA
SS: Hitler’s elite personal guard, who killed the SA leaders. SS was led by Heirich Himmler and took over the police and concentration camps.
Chamberlain: British Prime Minister that constantly gave Hitler territory because of his policy of appeasement, which was ultimately one of the causes of World War 2.
Henri-Philippe Petain: leader of Vichy France, the unoccupied area of France after German invasion in WW2
The Allies: Mainly France, Great Britain, US, Russia.
The Axis: Italy, Germany, Japan.

Important Treaties/Plans

New Economic Policy (NEP): Established by Lenin after the Russian Revolution, it was an economic reform to re-establish limited economic freedom and to rebuild agriculture and industry. Peasant farmers allowed to sell in free markets, private traders and handicraft manufacturers reappeared.
Five-Year Plans: Stalin’s response to the NEP, he wanted to limit economic freedom to avoid restoration of capitalism. He also wished to promote working classes and take away peasant privileges of land.
Collectivization: Stalin’s plan of seizing peasant lands by force and combining them into a state controlled enterprise for farming. Basically agricultural factories. In response many peasants burned their farms and killed their livestock.
Lateran Agreement: Mussolini recognized the Vatican as an independent state, and agreed to give the church money in return for the pope’s support of the new Fascist government.
Mein Kampf: Written by Hitler, he claimed Germans were the master race and needed to defend its pure blood by ridding of other racial classes in Germany. Claim Germans needed living space, which could be found in the East. He emphasized the idea of a leader-dictator, the Fuhrer, who would embody the people’s will and lead Germany into victory
Enabling Act: Granted Hitler dictatorial power for four years.
Appeasement: Great Britain’s policy of giving Hitler whatever he wanted to avoid war. Motivated by fear of the repeated outcome of WW1.

Main Events

Stalin’s Great Purges: it began when Stalin showered insults on her wife and she committed suicide, which was followed by the mysterious murder of Sergei Kirov. Stalin used the incident to launch a reign of terror to purge traitors. Stalin secret police executed the 16 men of the Old Bolsheviks, and held trials and executed other party members and even citizens. Even though the purges weakened the Soviet Union, it gave Stalin more control over the state.
Mussolini and Black Shirts march into Rome: In October 1922, a band of armed Fascists marched into Rome and threatened the king to appoint Mussolini as prime minister of Italy. Victor Emmanuel III, king at that time, gave in because he had no love for the liberal regime and allowed Mussolini to take over the government.
Hitler became Chancellor: During the Great Depression, Hitler promised economic and political salvation. It appealed to the middle and working class. Moreover, Chancellor Heinrich Bruning made a mistake by trying to overcome the crisis by cutting government spending and forcing down prices and wages. It made republican leaders seem corrupt and stupid, turning the people’s support to Hitler. In the Reichstag, the Communist Party and the Socialist Party hated each other, so they never overtook Hitler’s growing popularity. He gained support of conservative politicians in the Reichstag and he was appointed chancellor.
Spanish Civil War: Francisco Franco was leading a Fascist revolution in Spain. Italy and Germany decided to help Franco, and the Soviet Union helped the republic of Spain to fight back.
Hitler takes territory with appeasement: First Hitler left the League of Nations and violated the Treaty of Versailles by rebuilding the German military. Great Britain did nothing in response. He rebuilt the Rhineland, again violating the Treaty of Versailles, and yet GB and France still did nothing. He first took Austria and added it to Germany. Then he wanted Czechoslovakia, which was protected by France and the Soviet Union. THey came to an agreement and allowed Hitler to take Sudetenland, but not all of Czechoslovakia. However Hitler violated the agreement and took Czechoslovakia. Next he wanted Danzig, Poland, and this time Great Britain and France declared war on him when Hitler took Poland.
Japan Empire and War in Pacific: Japan had become a Fascist government and was taking territory in China. After joining the Axis, Japan desired a Great East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere, and began taking the colonies that Great Britain and the US controlled in the Pacific. They launched a surprise attack on Pearl Harbor, which incited the US to join the war. America and Australia fought against Japan in the Pacific to gain back their colonies, using an island-hopping campaign.
The Holocaust: The most devastating genocide in history, initiated by Nazi germany to rid Europe of Jews in order to “purify” Europe. Jews were forced to move into ghettos, which had very bad conditions. Soon extermination camps were built, the most famous in Auschwitz-Birkenau. Over 6 million Jews were killed in the holocaust.
Battle of Britain: Germany sought to control air over Britain, se they constantly bombed British airfields and factories, halting their supply of air forces. However, Hitler made the mistake of bombing British cities to break morale, but it allowed the increased production of aircraft to fight back against the Germans, and eventually the British came out victorious with 3-1 in air battles.
Warsaw Uprising: The Soviet Union reached Warsaw Germany, and anticipating defeat, the POlish underground Home Army ordered an uprising so that it could take Poland back on their own and establish independence from the Soviets. However, it was a miscalculation, as the Red Army refused to enter the city, which left the Polish to be killed off by the Germans.
End of the War: Finally germany was overwhelmed on all fronts, and hitler committed suicide, and german commanders surrendered. In the Pacific, Japan was losing heavily, but refused to surrender. After the US dropped the atomic bomb on Hiroshima and Nagasaki, Japan finally surrendered, and the war was over, with a total of 50 million deaths.
