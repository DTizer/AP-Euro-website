Chapter 21: Idealogies and Upheavals

In this chapter, you will learn about the ideologies of nationalism, and how it motivated the revolutions presented in this chapter, especially the revolution of 1848, which is a key event in European history that marks the shift from an absolute monarchy to a republic government.

Quizlet Flashcards

Main Ideas

Conservatism: cultural, social, and political ideology that seeks to preserve or go back to traditional customs and culture. This idea opposed many forms of change, such as liberalism and socialism.
Liberalism: demanded representative government as opposed to autocratic monarchy, and equality before the law as opposed to legally separate classes. Also desired individual freedoms.
Nationalism: ideology and vision of people united together by religion, language, common history, and culture. Influence of nationalism often led to people’s desire for independence, leading to revolutions.
Socialism: more radical than liberalism and nationalism, socialism looks to improve life of the lower classes and advocates for economic changes that would promote free market and equality. They also believed in state regulation of property or community ownership.
Laissez faire: doctrine that called for free trade, unrestricted private enterprise, and no government interference in the economy
Marxism: Founded by Karl Marx, it was the idea that social uprising of the lower classes of the proletariat would overthrow the class position of the bourgeoisie. This idea was fueled by the belief class differences have played a big role in economic and social life throughout history. When the proletariat finally overthrew the bourgeoisie, it would lead to an age of communism, a system of radical equality.
Romanticism: An artistic movement in reaction to the enlightenment, it emphasized emotions of love, happiness, hatred, sadness, and rejected reason and rationalism. It also rejected the secular idea of the enlightenment, and instead embraced religion. Romanticists valued intuition and history.

Important People/Groups

Quadruple Alliance: Alliance between Russia, Prussia, Austria. and Great Britain that worked together to maintain the balance of power of France.
Metternich: He was a very conservative and important figure during this time period. During the congress of Vienna, he opposed many liberal desires and defended the monarchical status quo. He has a very pessimistic view of human nature and resented liberalism. He was afraid that liberalism would lead to increased nationalism and aspirations for independent government, leading to revolutions across Europe. As a foreign minister of Austria, he opposed independent governments in his own country because of Austria’s heavily divided cultures and society, with many ethnic races living in Austria.
Louis XVIII: restored to the throne after the fall of Napoleon and by the decision of the Quadruple alliance made at the Congress of Vienna.
Holy Alliance: Alliance between Austria, Prussia, and Russia, meant to repress reformist and revolutionary movements for independence across Europe.
Alexander I: Tsar of Russia from 1801-1825, he supported Metternich’s conservative views and also opposed liberalism and spread of nationalism.
Nicholas I: After the death of Alexander I, Nicholas I took the throne from 1825-1855, and he had to deal with an uprising in St Petersburg in December 1825.
Creoles: direct descendants of Spanish parents born in america
Simon Bolivar: led the north of south america in revolution against the spanish, and he came out victorious, establishing a Gran Colombia which was inspired by the United states to unite the other south american states.
Henri de Saint-Simon: he believed the key to social progress was for the “parasites” — aristocracy, lawyers, churchmen, to give way to the “doers” — leading scientists, engineers, and industrialists.
Charles Fourier: envisioned a utopian society with precise phalanxes, each made up of 1620 people. He supported emancipation of women and wanted women to freely find love.
Robert Owen: he was an early promoter of labor unions and called for society to be reorganized into model industrial-agricultural communities.
Pierre-Joseph Proudhon: argued that property of the working class is their labor, and their labor was being exploited for profit. He believed states should be abolished and society made of loose organization of working people.
Louis Blanc: In Organizations of Work, he urged the working class to fight for universal voting rights and to take control of the state peacefully. Blanc believed the state should set up government funded workshops to guarantee employment for everyone.
Karl Marx: Founder of the idea of Marxism, as explained above.
Wordsworth: he wrote poetry and believed everything was sacred, expressing a mystical appearance of nature in his writings.
Coleridge: published Lyrical Ballads, which abandoned flowery classical conventions for language of ordinary speech and endowed simple subjects with majesty.
Stael: she championed the new movement of romanticism in France, and urged french to throw away worn out classical models.
Hugo: He supported liberty in politics and society in his writings. He expressed romantic fashions of humans, emotions, and setting. Showed that romanticism was compatible with many political beliefs.
Delacroix: He was inspired by exotic objects and painted dramatic colorful scenes that stirred emotions.
Casper David Freidrich: painted somber landscapes to capture the divine presence in natural forces.
Turner: he depicted nature’s power and terror by painting wild storms and sinking ships
Constable: painted landscapes in which humans live peacefully in the countryside.
Franz Liszt: very famous and well known pianist during the romantic period.
Beethoven: used contrasting themes and tones to produce dramatic conflict and inspiring resolutions.
Ypsilanti: Led the Greek Revolution which became a success. He was also a general in the Russian army.
Tories: They were the conservative party of Great Britain at this time, and opposed the Whig Party. This party was also made up of aristocrats.
Whig Party: Even though they were also made up of aristocrats, they supported the middle class. They were more liberal and opposed the Tories.
Anti-Corn Law League: Made of working class middle class reformers, they desired universal male suffrage and the repeal of the Corn Laws, which hindered their ability to work.
Robert Peel: he moved from the Tories to the Whig party and helped repeal the corn laws in 1846
Irish: they competed with the British, and many Irish people rented land from English landowners, which led to them being exploited for profit. The Irish relied on potato crops for food because they sold their other crops for money. One acre of potatoes could feed a family of 6. However, the potato crop would later cost them, leading to the Great Famine.
Charles X: he was a reactionary and was very conservative, wanting to reestablish monarchical rule in France. His attempt to remove the Constitutional charter led to a revolution, discussed later.
Louis Phillipe: After Charles X failure, Louis Phillpe took over and accepted the Constitutional Charter, and further built France into a republic.
Alexis de Tocqueville: He explained the results of the election of France’s second republic was because of socialist movements in Paris that aroused the peasants, middle, and upper classes. He believed that when one owns land, like the peasants, they become more united together and act together.
Constituent Assembly: New Assembly based on election in the Second France Republic.
Ferdinand I: Emperor of Habsburg Empire that included Austria, which was facing revolutions at this time.

Francis Joseph: Crowned emperor of Austria form 1848-1916
FrankFurt Parliament: National Parliament of the German Confederation, tasked to develop a new constitution that would unite the states of the confederation.
Greater Germany: In the constitution, it proposed unification of a Greater Germany that would include German speaking lands of the Austrian Empire.
Lesser Germany: With a greater Germany, lesser Germany was Prussia and the rest of the German states, without Austria.
Frederick William IV: When the constitution was proposed, the parliament elected Fredrick William IV, current ruler of Prussia, to be emperor of Lesser Germany. However he refused, saying he did not want a “crown from the gutter” Secreetly, he did want to become emperor, but on his own terms, however Austria rejected that. So the Frankfurt Parliament dissolved and the German states remained split.

Important Treaties/Plans

Congress system: Agreement of the Quadruple Alliance to meet periodically to maintain peace in Europe. This was known as the congress system, which lasted into the late 19th century.
Karlsbad Decrees: required German states to outlaw liberal organizations, police universities and newspapers, and establish a committee of spies to clamp down liberal or radical reformers.
Corn Laws: prohibited the importation of making foreign grain in Great Britain, making the bread prices very high, benefitting aristocrats.
Six Acts: Passed by Parliament, it placed heavy taxes on the press and eliminated mass meetings. This was in response to uprisings against the New Corn Laws.
Reform Bill of 1832: moved British politics in a democratic direction and created a House of Commons and a House of Lords.
Ten Hours Act: Limited workday for women and children in factories to 10 hours. Competing with the Whigs for support of the middle class, the Tories passed this act to gain support of the working class.

Main Events

Congress of Vienna: Meeting of the Quadruple Alliance to restore monarchy to France and reestablish peace by distributing territory lost after the Napoleonic Wars. Austria gave up territories in Belgium and southern Germany but took provinces of Venetia and Lombardy in northern Italy as well as former Polish possessions and new lands on the eastern coast of the Adriatic. When France, Austria, and Great Britain all argued for limited gain, Russia accepted a small Polish kingdom and Prussia took only part of Saxony in addition to its gains to the west
Revolutions in Spain and Italy: revolutions in Spain and Italy came out successful, so Alexander I called for the principle of active intervention to maintain autocratic regimes when threatened. Hence, Austrian forces restored power for Ferdinand I in Sicily and France invaded Spain to return power to the king.
Greek Revolution 1821: The Greeks had always been controlled by the Ottoman Empire, but rising nationalism in Greece led to desire for independence. Led by Ypsiltani, the Greek revolution gained support because many European countries and Romanticists saw Greece as a place of origin of classical culture of the Renaissance. Britain, France, and Russia offered the Ottoman empire an armistice, but they refused, so their navies trapped the Ottoman fleet at Narivano and declared Greece an independent state.
Battle of Peterloo: Government violently repressed an uprising in Great Britain against the Corn Laws. It was a scornful reference to British victory at the Battle of Waterloo during Napoleonic Wars.
Great Famine: A disease spread in the Irish potato crops, which led to famine in Ireland. Great Britain did not offer much assistance, and still continued to tax and exploit the Irish. The Great Famine promoted Irish nationalism and anti-British feeling, leading to future revolutions in the 20th century.
Revolution of France 1830: Charles X wanted to restore French monarchical rule, so he needed to gain support and remove the Constitutional Charter. He decided the best way was to incite the French into a war, and a diplomatic dispute with Algeria was the perfect thing. French forces of 37k invaded Algeria, and took the capital city in 3 weeks. Happy with the results, Charles repudiated the Constitutional Charter in an attempted coup. The following reaction was a violent revolution, which caused Charles X to flee. His cousin, Louis Phillipe, took his place as the new ruler of France.
France Second Republic and Revolution: Louis Phillipe’s rule was corrupted by the wealthy bourgeoisie, which caused stagnation and decline in agriculture. This caused the people to be mad, and they demanded a republic, uprising in the streets of Paris by building barricades. The National Guard, supposed to put down the revolution, instead joined them. Louis Napoleon wanted to abdicate in favor of his grandson, but the people did not want another monarchy. To quell the uprising, moderate republicans set up national workshops, and then later created the Constituent Assembly, which had 900 seats, and the election results came out to have 500 monarchists, which angered the people once again. Following that, Blanc was dropped, angering the socialists in France. The assembly continued to dissolve the national workshops, leading once again to uprising and barricades in Paris. A class war began, known as 3 terrible “June Days”, with the republican army coming out on top. The constituent assembly was re-elected, and this time Louis Napoleon won the election, leading to a semi authoritarian government in France.
Austrian Revolution 1848: Seeing the reactions and revolution in France, liberals in Austria also demanded civil liberties from authoritarian regimes. Viennese students built barricades, and peasants broke out in uprising across the empire. Ferdinand I abolished serfdom, and the peasants lost interest in revolution. However, ethnic differences in Hungary led to nationalism and rising desires for independence. Finally, archduchess Sophia and conservative aristocrats bombarded Prague and crushed the working class revolt. Heavy artillery used against the Vienna students finally ended the revolution. Francis joseph was then crowned as emperor.
