Chapter 20: The Revolution in Energy and Industry

This chapter will talk about the most important change in life during the period of AP Euro, which is the Industrial Revolution. You will learn about the pioneers of the revolution and all the new innovations involved, and how the Industrial revolution began and how it will continue to affect life after and in the future.

Quizlet Flashcards

Main Ideas

Putting out system: merchant loan raw materials to cottage workers who then processed the materials into goods and give them back for the merchant to sell for a profit
Iron law of wages: Made by David Ricardo, said that over time, as the population grows, wages will decrease to the level that it's just high enough to keep workers from starving.
Tariff Protection: it was a policy first adopted by Britain to protect their goods and prevent competition, but was soon adopted by the rest of the European states. It was basically you put taxes on your imports.
Zollverein: a German customs union, it allowed goods to move between member states without tariffs, and also had a single high tariff on outside nations.
Separate spheres: Men were the primary wage earners while women had very little job opportunities and were expected to concentrate on house chores.

New inventions

Spinning Jenny: it was inexpensive and powered by hand. six to twenty-four spindles were mounted on a sliding carriage, and each spindle spun a fine, slender thread. The machines were usually worked by women, who moved the carriage back and forth with one hand and turned a wheel to supply power with the other. Now it was the male weaver who could not keep up with the vastly more efficient female spinner.
Water Frame: it had a capacity of several hundred spindles and was powered by water movement, but it could only spin, strong, coarse thread.
Mule Hybrid Machine: It was the most used and most efficient, able to spin both fine and strong thread in large quantities.
Power loom: it did not require hand labor but it did not work well at first, and only replaced handlooms in the 1820s.
Steam Engine: it burned coal to produce steam that would power a machine. It was very revolutionary and provided power to many machines that made labor easier.
Puddling Furnace: As ironmakers began to use coke, a substance produced by heating coal to make pig iron, blast furnaces were built to increase efficiency, and then the puddling furnace was made, allowing pig iron to be refined in turn with coke.
Steam Locomotive: it was a train that used the power of steam engines, and revolutionized transportation of goods and led to many railroads built across Europe.

Important Acts

Factory Acts: progressively limited the workday of child laborers and set hygiene and safety requirements. Children between ages nine and thirteen could work a maximum of eight hours per day, not including two hours that must be devoted to education. Teenagers aged fourteen to eighteen could work up to twelve hours, while those under nine were banned from employment.
Mines Act of 1842: prohibited underground work for all women and girls as well as for boys under ten.
Combination Acts: passed by Parliament, it outlawed unions and strikes in Britain.
