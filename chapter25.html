Chapter 25: War and Revolution

In this chapter, we will explore the causes of WW1, how diplomatic relations between European countries soured and how the tensions to gain Balkan lands ultimately caused the war. We will then discuss the events during war and the idea of total war, followed by the end of the war and the peace treaties and alliances that followed. In addition, we will learn about the Russian Revolution and Russian Civil War, and its impact on Russia in the future.

Main Ideas

Total War: Total War is the idea that not only did the military of the country take part in the war, so did the rest of the country, providing economic and industrial supplies to the battlefront. Government often intervened with the economy in the country and many men were sent to the battle front, leaving women to take over their jobs.
Trench Warfare: Because of new deadly weapons like the Maxim machine gun, soldier could not directly charge at enemies because they would get shredded. Instead, both sides dug long and deep trenches to protect themselves, leading to heavy stalemate on the Western side of the war. When a soldier walks out of the trench and crosses no man’s land, the land between trenches, that is called “going over the top”
Unrestricted submarine warfare: Germany competed with Great Britain by building its own nay fleet after the 1900s. They built one of the newest war machines, the submarine, and used it to sink American ships providing supplies to Britain and France. America got mad and Germany stopped using submarines to avoid war with the US. HOwever, close to the end of the war, Germany used their submarines in hopes that they could starve Britain before the US could help them. This caused the US to join in WW1.
National self determination: An idea proposed by President Wilson in his fourteen points, which meant that people should be able to choose their own national governments through elections and live free from outside interference.

Important People/Groups/Alliances

Triple Alliance: Alliance between Austria, Germany, and Italy.
Triple Entente: Alliance between France, Great Britain, and Russia
The Allies: Later in the war, the US joins the Entente and Italy switches sides, creating the alliance of France, Great Britain, Russia, US and Italy, known as the Allies.
Central Power: Austria, Germany, and the Ottoman Empire.
William II: He disliked Bismarck’s kind relations with the other countries and rather desired war, so he kicked Bismarck and refused to renew the non aggression pact with Russia.
Archduke Francis Ferdinand: Heir to the Austro-Hungarian Throne, he was assassinated by Gravilo Princip, which caused the beginnings of the Balkan Wars and ultimately led to WW1
Hussein ibn-Ali: He was the Arabs leader during the war, the chief magistrate of Mecca, and managed to help the British win commitments which earned him an independent Arab kingdom.
T.E. Lawrence: He was a British officer, and he helped Ali in a successful guerilla war against the Turks in 1917.
Fatherland Party: Germany’s generals decided to rule by decree, and they formed the Fatherland Party, which was a conservative party that helped establish a military dictatorship.
Paul von Hindenburg & Erich Ludendorff: Germany’s generals who drove chancellor Bethmann-Hollweg from office and created a new government.
Liebknecht: He was a radical socialist leader that attacked the costs of the war effort, leading him to be imprisoned.
Clemenceau: France Prime minister, he established a dictatorship and arrested people who opposed the war effort. He also demanded Germany face harsh punishments after WW1 to pay for causing the war.
Petain: One of the more important French Generals at the time of the war.
Nicholas II: The tsar of Russia during WW1, he embraced the war and vowed never to make peace as long as enemies stood on Russian soil. However, Russia was facing large defeats and the people were losing morale. Nicholas relied on bureaucracies rather than the newly elected Duma, which angered the people, so he temporarily put off the Duma, and left to fight the war at the front.
Tsarina Alexandra: With Ncholas II gone, he put his wife in charge, who was not a good leader and instead relied on the unpopular and distrusted Rasputin.
Rasputin: He was known for being able to heal Alexis, Alexandra’s only son, from his hemophilia. Because he was distrusted and was corrupting the government, he was murdered by aristocrats, leaving Russia in shambles. In 1917 the Duma declared a provisional government, leading to Nicholas II abdication 3 days later.
Provisional Government: Led by Alexander Kerensky, the provisional government was generally moderately socialist, and it wanted to continue the war.
Petrograd Soviet: Competitor of the provisional government, it was a council of soldiers and workers, and it issued its own radical orders to weaken the authority of the provisional government.
Lenin: He became an enemy of Russia after his brother was executed for plotting to kill the tsar. He studied Marxist socialism, and his ideas stemmed from there. He believed only a violent revolution could destroy capitalism, and he also believed that a revolution was possible even with Russia, because the peasants could take the place of Marx’s traditional working class. He also believed the possibility of a revolution is determined by human leadership and not historical laws. He wanted a small workers’ party controlled by elite revolutionaries.
Bolsheviks: “majority group” Lenin led this party, and the Bolsheviks desired a small elite party dedicated to a Communist Revolution.
Mensheviks: “minority group” the Mensheviks desired a more democratic reformist party with mass membership.
Kornilov: He was the Russian army’s commander in chief, and he led a coup against Kerensky and the provisional government, but the Bolsheviks pushed back the counter-revolutionary attempt, and Kerensky lost credit with the army, leaving the Bolsheviks to seize power.
Trotsky: Trotsky was Lenin’s number 1 supporter, and he helped establish a one party Bolsehvik rule. He convinced the Petrograd Soviet to form a military-revolutionary committee and make Trotsky their leader. So the Bolsheviks gained military power, and they seized government buildings in Petrograd and arrested members of the provisional government. During the Russian Civil War, Trotsky also led the Bolshevik’s army, the Red army.
White Army: The opposing army towards the Bolsheviks during the civil war. They were only united by their hatred of communism and the Bolsheviks. However, they lost the war because they were highly uncoordinated and did not have a leader.
Red Army: The bolshevik’s army in the civil war, led by Trotsky. They won the war because they had a better army, a good leader, and they were very disciplined.
Germany Social Democratic Party: The authoritarian monarchy was overthrown, but the social democrats established a democratic government called the Weimar Republic. The social democratic parties were more moderate and not so much revolutionaries.
Weimar Republic: The democratic government after WW1 in Germany, which was really unstable because of its voting system that allowed many smaller parties to gain seats in the Reichstag. One of them was the Nazi Party, who eventually gained popular support and established their one party totalitarian rule.
Free Corp Militias: Radical Communists such as Liebknecht and Luxemburg tried to seize control of government in the Spartacist Uprising. They were easily stopped by the Free Corp Militias, a band of demobilized soldiers who had kept their weapons.
Big Three: France, represented by Clemecuaue, US, represented by President Wilson, and Great Britain, represented by Llyod George.
Arthur Balfour: British Foreign secretary who wrote the Balfour declaration.
Mustafa Kemal: He led the Turkish resistance towards the Allies attempts to split Turkey, which was eventually successful and Turkey became an independent state free from foreign suppression or control.

Important Treaties/Plans

Germany-Russia Insurance Treaty: It was a defensive pact between Germany and Russia before WW1, which stated that if one of the countries got attacked, the other would remain neutral and not attack them.
Blank Check: Because of the Balkan conflict and after Austria declared war on Serbia, Germany realized that it might have to go on a war on two fronts with Russia and Austria. Hence, Germany promised Austria that it would be an ally to Austria, which was signed on a blank check, and encouraged Austria to attack Serbia.
Schlieffen Plan: It was a plan to take over France, by first going through Britain, and it was gambling on the fact that Russia would be too slow to mobilize troops in the east. Because Belgium was neutral, Germany hoped to walk through Belgium, but they resisted, so Germany had to invade Belgium. This caused Great Britain to declare war on Germany, marking the beginning of World War 1.
Auxiliary Service Law: It was imposed by Germany, which required all males between 17 and 60 to work only at jobs critical to the war effort.
Army Order No. 1: Imposed by the Petrograd Soviet, it stripped officers of their authority and placed power in the hands of elected committees of common soldiers.
Treaty of Brest-Litovsk: Signed by Russia, they left the war, giving up 1/3 of its territory. It was imposed by Lenin, and now with Russia out of war, he could carry out the revolution to give power to the Bolsheviks.
War Communism: It was a system of controls established by the Bolsheviks during the Civil war, so that they controlled industries and economy to gain supplies and eventually win the war.
Treaty of Versailles: The treaty that established peace and laid out terms of settlement with Germany after the end of the war. It forced Germany to pay large reparations. Germany had to give up their colonies to France and Britain, and in the treaty, the war guilt clause stated that Austria and Germany are responsible for starting the war, and hence they have to pay reparations.
Fourteen Points: President Wilson’s peace proposal, which laid out 14 terms such as open diplomacy, reduction of armaments, freedom of trade and commerce, establishment of League of Nations, and national self-determination for countries. Note that only one of the 14 points was actually included in the treaty, which was the establishment of a League of Nations.
League of Nations: It was an international body designed to provide a place for peaceful resolution of international problems.
Mandate system: an agreement between Britain and France that former Ottoman territories would be administered by the European Powers.
Balfour Declaration: announced that Britain favored a “National Home for the Jewish People” in Palestine. Basically established Palestine as a home for the Jews.

Main Events

First Moroccan Crisis 1905: After seeing that France and Britain were allied, William II of Germany tried to break it by declaring Morocco, a place where France had colonial interests, independent. It only brought France and Britain closer together. Note that this was not a form of colonial competition between Germany and Britain in the 1900s. Colonial competition between Germany and Britain was before the 1900s.
First Balkan War: After the Ottoman Empire gave up the states of Serbia, Bulgaria, and Albania, these states desired independence and were feeling nationalistic. In 1912, Serbia joined Greece and Bulgaria to attack the Ottoman Empire.
Second Balkan War: Bulgaria attacked its former allies, leading Austria to intervene who forced Serbia to give up Albania.
Third Balkan War: After the assassination of Archduke Francis Ferdinand, Austria gave Serbia an ultimatum, but Serbia replied evasively, leading Austria to declare war on Serbia. This became the First World War.
Battle of Verdun: The French and British attacked the German front, which only led to heavy casualties and barely any territory gained.
Battle of the Somme: The British attempted to break the German line with heavy artillery and gunfire. The battle ended up with the British pushing the Germans back 7 miles, at the cost of lots of casualties.
Armenian Genocide: Fighting between Ottomans and Russians led the Armenians to be involved, who were heavily reppresed by the Ottomans. Armenians welcomed Russians as liberators, but the Ottoman ordered a mass deportation of Armenians, leading to the death of 1 million Armenians.
Battle of Gallipoli: The British tried to take Dardanelles and Constantinople from the Ottomans. The invasion failed, leading to a 10 month long battle on the beaches and a lot of casualties.
Irish Easter Rising: Armed republican militias took over Dublin and declared an independent Irish Republic. British troops crushed the rebels and their leaders, marking the failure of the revolution. However, it set the stage for success of the Sinn Fein Party and civil war for eventual Irish Independence in 1920
February Revolution: t was an unplanned uprising of hungry, and angry people in the capital of Russia.
October Revolution: Revolution by the Bolsheviks as they took over government buildings and arrested Petrogard and Provisional government members.
