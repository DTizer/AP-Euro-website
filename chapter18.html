Chapter 18: Life in the Era of Expansion

In this chapter, you will learn about the ways of life of different classes and genders as the population grows, the revival of religions, and life as the rise of the consumer revolution and agricultural revolution continues. Because this chapter covers a lot of general facts, this page will only cover important terms to know

Important Terms

Nuclear Families: As more ways of life emerged, many families became the typical family of parents and their children, which is known as nuclear families. If families had 3 generations living together, that was mostly because a widowed parent moved into the home of a married child.
Community Controls: this help limit the number of illegitimate births, especially with the increase of premarital sex. Village elders, priests, and landlords put pressure on the younger people who were uncertain about marriage when faced with pregnancies. A humiliating public ritual was used as a community control, called the charivari, where young men would force him or her to sit on a donkey facing backward and holding up the donkey’s tail, and march around the town.
Illegitimacy explosion: In the second half of the 18th century, community controls were loosened, which led to a rise of illegitimate births.
Wet Nursing: it was the role of wet nurses, who were women that were paid to breastfeed a mother’s baby. However, reliance on wet nurses increased infant mortality, because they were transported around, and also the conditions of life during this time were very crowded and not sanitary.
Blood Sports: bull baiting, cockfighting, it was a form of leisure activity for the working class/ middle class to watch.
Just Price: advocated by peasants, landless laborers, and urban workers, they believed prices should be fair and protect consumers and producers, and they should be imposed by the government when necessary.
Consumer Revolution: goods increased in variety, which led to a growth of consumption of goods. This led to people having new forms of self-identity, individuality and self expression.
Pietism: it was the protestant revival in germany. It called for warm emotional religion everyone would experience, extending bible reading and importance to all classes, and believed in the power of Chirstian rebirth.
Methodists: organized by John Wesley, he was at first skeptical about his salvation because the Church of England was corrupted and exploited. But after he met a Pietist, he was transformed and went around preaching its ideas, rejecting calvinist ideas of predestination and instead said anyone can seek salvation.
Jansenism: founded by Cornelius Jansen, it emphasized the heavy weight of original sin and accepted the idea of predestination. It attracted a lot of Catholics, especially the French.
