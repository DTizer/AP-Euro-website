Chapter 24: The West and the World

This chapter explores the growing population of Europe and Western countries during the 1800s. We also explore relations in trade between Europe and Asia, the causes of European imperialism, and the countries that Europe took control over.

Main Ideas:

Imperialism: European nations exercised political and economic control over Africa and Asia. In some places they established protectorates, where the dependent country had its own government but was still subject to the authority of the imperial power.
Gunboat diplomacy: the use or threat of military force to compel a government into economic or political agreements.
Nativism: the policy of protecting established inhabitants against those of immigrants.
White man’s burden: America's belief that they had the benefits to bestow on supposedly less advanced people.
Orientalism: a term to describe the fascination and the stereotypical and often racist Western understandings of non-Westerners that dominated Western thought
Social Darwinism: the idea that human beings are subjected to the same law of natural selection. Social Darwinism is used to justify imperialism.

Imperialized Countries

China: China has always sent a lot of goods and inventions to Europe, and trade is carefully regulated by the imperial government. However, the British began smuggling opium to China and selling them illegally. British merchants in China soon wanted a British colony there for unrestricted trade. The Chinese government decided to stamp out the opium trade, but the British responded by occupying China’s coastal cities, which led to the Opium Wars.
Japan: Back in the 1600, Japan resented the presence of foreigners so they decided to expel all of them and seal Japan from European influences in order to preserve their culture. Japan’s isolation threatened the United States, and the US felt they should force the Japanese to open their ports for trade. Matthew Perry went to Edo, and with gunboat diplomacy, he managed to get the Japanese to sign a treaty with the US to open their trading ports.
Egypt: Muhammad Ali hired French and Italian army officers to train new recruits of peasants in the army. He also reformed the government, cultivated new lands, and improved communication networks. He also encouraged development of commercial agriculture. His son, Ismail, continued in his father’s steps and advocated many reforms and built many projects. The French completed the Suez Canal for him in 1869. With many expensive projects, Ismail had to take loans from France and Great Britain, which eventually led France and Great Britain to take control of Egypt’s finances.
Africa: Britain, led by Cecil Rhodes, took control of Orange Free State and the Transval. King Leopold II sent Henry M. Stanley to the Congo basin, where he managed to establish relations and sign treaties to ensure Belgium’s control. France saw this and then sent Pierre de Brazza to establish a protectorate north bank of Congo river
India: British Parliament controlled India. It decided to make some changes by providing secondary education all in English, which provided economic and social opportunities for Hindus. Britain also improved economic development by building projects. They united Hindus and muslims. However, despite the improvements, the native people were still not seen as equals to the whites and were still discriminated against. Indian Nationalism was rising, which led to the Indian National congress to call for independence.

Important People/Groups

Neo-Europes: describes regions that already have significant populations of ethnic Europes, which included US, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Latin America, and Siberia.
Matthew Perry: Led the US into gunboat diplomacy with Japan, and managed to get Japan to sign a treaty that would open their ports to foreign trade.
Muhammad Ali: Governor of Egypt during the period of Imperialism.
Ismail: Muhammad Ali’s son, who took on costly projects, leading to Europe control of Egypt’s finance.
Irish and Jewish emigrants: They often left their country to avoid discrimination and oppression, and usually when they left they never went back.
Afrikaners: Descendants of the Dutch in the Cape Colony
Cecil Rhodes: Led the British imperialism of Africa.
Leopold II: King of Belgium and appointed Henry M. Stanley to establish Belgian control over the Congo.
Henry M. Stanley: Established belgian control of Congo
Edward Said: coined the term orientalism
Hobson: Critiqued Imperialism. He said the rush to acquire colonies was due to economic need of capitalism. He said imperialism did not actually benefit the home country economically. Only certain groups benefited off imperials. He also said that imperialism diverted attention away from domestic reform and the need to reduce the gap between rich and poor.

Important Treaties/Plans

Treaty of Nanking: the imperial government of China was required to cede the island of Hong Kong to the British and pay an indemnity of $100 millions, and open up 4 large cities to unlimited foreign trade with low tariffs.
Berlin Conference: Called by Jules Ferry and Bismarck, it established the principle that European claims to AFrican territory had to rest on “effective occupation” to be recognized by other states.
