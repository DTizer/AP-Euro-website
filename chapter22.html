Chapter 22: Life in the Emerging Urban Society

In this chapter, you will learn about the increasing population in the Western World, which led to new life changes in society and the Scientific Revolution. You will also learn about emerging gender roles and the improving lives of the working class. With these benefits, there are also costs, which you will also explore in the chapter.

Chapter Questions

Main Ideas

Utilitarianism: ethical dilemmas or public problems ought to be dealt with on a rational, scientific basis to advanced the “greatest good for the greatest number”
Companionate Marriage: marriage based on affection, friendship, and sexual gratification
Separate Spheres: the typical idea that the wife should be the mother and take care of home domestic affairs, while the husband is the wage earner and breadwinner
Theory of Evolution: we all evolve from the same organism, and variations in species are because of natural selection for who can survive the fittest.
Social Darwinism: global competition between countries is a grand struggle for for survival, and this idea was used to justify imperialism and racism
Realism: depicted life exactly as it was. Used in art and literature.

Important People/Groups

Working Class: Working class enjoyed leisure time by drinking, watching sports and performances in music halls. They watched sports such as cockfighting and bull-baiting. Music halls included songs with jokes of drunkenness, premarital sex, and marital difficulties. Working class also followed religion and attended church, but there was definitely a decline from before. This was because church construction failed to keep up with rapid industrialization, and they were seen as conservative institutions. It was also thought that more women should attend church than men.
Bentham: introduced the idea of utilitarianism
Chadwick: he believed the government can help prevent disease by cleaning up urban areas, and installing a sewage system to clean up wastes would be good.
Pasteur: he studied the process of fermentation and discovered that activity of bacteria can be suppressed by heating them up, a process which was soon named pasteurization. The implication of his discovery was that living organisms that cause diseases can be controlled.
Koch: he developed cultures of harmful bacteria and his discovery led to development of vaccines.
Lister: he connected bacterial influence on wound infections, and discovered the method of sterilizing equipment, especially in hospitals.
Hausmann: Place by Napoleon III to redesign Paris. He made the streets wider so that it would be harder for protesters to make a blockade. He also installed two parks and open spaces throughout the city.
White-collar employees: a mixed group of traveling salesmen, bookkeepers, store managers, and clerks who staffed the offices and branch stores of large corporations.
Labor aristocracy: highly skilled workers that made up 15% of the working class. They earned double the earnings of unskilled laborers.
Domestic servants: mostly women and they received very low pay and faced the danger of sexual exploitation. Took care of domestic household stuff and did chores for the house. They had the benefits of having better marriage options and their wages were still higher than hard work in agriculture.
Sweated Industries: ranks of working class women and they had to work because the wages were too low to support the family with the husband only working.
Gustav Droz: urged fathers to be more affectionate toward their children and following more affection advice, mothers increasingly breastfeed their infants.
Wollstonecraft: advocated for women's rights and supported the feminist movement.
Newton: invented laws of thermodynamics and laws of conservation of energy
Mendeleev: organized elements into the first periodic table. Science became more rigid and was the only reliable source to truth and reality, which supported Enlightenment ideas. Works of poets and saints and religion became inferior.
Lyell: his principle of uniformitarianism states that the same geological processes at work today are what formed earth's surface over time.
Lamarck: asserted that all forms of life have evolved through a long process of adjustment to environment, a challenge to the belief in divine creation of species.

Important Treaties/Plans/Changes in Life

Contagious Disease Acts: women identified as “common prostitutes” and were required to be checked for diseases. If they had disease, they were locked in hospital and forced to undergo treatment. Led to a feminist movement in response.
Suffrage movement: Movement for women to be able to vote. Despite many protests, the British Parliament refused to allow women to vote until after WW1, in 1919.
German Civil Code 1906: granted women substantial gains in family law and property right
Second Industrial Revolution: With the discovery of electricity, new innovations like the electric streetcar improved way of life, by giving public transport.
