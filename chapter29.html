Chapter 29: Challenging the Postwar Order

In this chapter, you will learn about the resolution to the Cold War, and the rise of new movements that challenged Cold War ideas and events, such as the counterculture movement, the feminist and environmentalist movement, and the new culture of youth, and finally learn about the Unification of Germany, once again.

Main Ideas

Ostpolitik: Willy Brandt’s policy of relaxing tensions between central Europe and German States after World War II.
Detente: general relaxation of east and west tensions.
New Left: advocated for a style of socialism that avoids capitalism and soviet style communism.
Samizdat: books, pamphlets, newspapers that were secretly published and passed hand to hand because it criticized communism which went beyond the limits of criticism accepted in the state.
Stagflation: describe combination of low growth and high inflation during the 1950s and 1960s.
Post Industrial society: new age of society that led to technological advances which streamlined production of goods, while heavy industry declined.
Neoliberalism: it was inspired by laissez faire free market ideas. Argued that they should cut support for social services, limit government subsidies, and retreat from regulation of all kinds. They also called for privatization.
Privatization: the sale of state managed industries to private owners. This will help tighten government spending and lead to greater workplace efficiency.
Separatism: the idea of many regional ethnic groups that fought special rights and political autonomy separate from their Communist governments
Developed socialism: Used by communist leaders in eastern europe and the Soviet Union to describe the accomplishments of their societies.
Perestroika: Gorbachev’s reform of economic restructuring. He permitted easing of government price controls, independence for state enterprises, and creation of profit seeking private cooperatives to provide personal services.
Glasnost: Gorbachev’s new campaign for openness in government and media.

Important People/Groups

Willy Brandt: First Social Democratic West German Chancellor. He had the policy of Ostpolitik, to ease tensions in Central Europe and postwar German states. He fell to his knees in front of a ghetto memorial and asked for pardon for the Germany atrocities in WWII.
Christa Wolf: author of Divided Heaven
Alexander Dubcek: part of the Prague Spring reforms, he became the communist leader in Czechoslovakia. He believed he could reconcile genuine socialism with personal freedom and party democracy. He remained loyal to the Warsaw Pact, but the Soviet Leaders still feared that Czechoslovakia would be drawn to NATO, so the Soviets put troops to occupy Czech and managed to stop the Czech reforms.
OPEC: Arab led organization of petroleum exporting countries
Margaret Thatcher: New conservative leader of Great Britain, she cut spending on health care, education, and public housing. She encouraged low and moderate income renters in state owned housing projects to buy their apartments at very low prices, and this created a new class of property owners. However her reforms were not very effective and led to lots of revolts, so she was replaced by John Major.
Ronald Reagan: another conservative leader, president of the United States, he cut taxes but failed to limit government spending, which led the US debt to increase a lot.
Helmut Kohl: Like Thatcher and Reagan, he was also a conservative and was chancellor of west germany, cutting taxes and government spending. This led to increased unemployment in heavy industry, but also led to solid economic growth.
Francois Mitternand: He led France on a turn towards the left. He launched programs of nationalization and public investment to get the country out of stagnation. However, his reforms failed and he was forced to place austerity measures.
Simone de Beauvoir: Wrote Second Sex, she argued that women had always been trapped by inflexible and limiting conditions. She analyzed women through the frame of existential thought, drawing on history, philosophy, psychology, biology, and literature.
Betty Friedan: she argued housewives live in a gilded cage because they are not allowed to hold professional jobs or become adults and genuine human beings. She also helped found the National Organization for Women.
Rachel Carson: she was an environmentalist and she wrote Silent Spring, which was about a society that would wake up and hear no birds singing because they had all been killed by pesticides.
ETA: a separatist group in Spain that tried to use bombings and and assassinations to force the government to grant them independence. When Francisco Franco died, the ETA was granted a new constitution but it was not enough, so they continued their terrorism.
IRA: Irish Republican army that fought for complete independence from Great Britain. On BloodySunday in 1972, the British army shot and killed 13 peaceful protesters, which led to the IRA to wreak havoc on soldiers and civilians for the next thirty years, which was known as the Troubles. Negotiations opened in 1990 and finally an agreement was reached in 1998.
Jean Marie Le Pen: he founded the French National front and opposed europe integration and called for return to traditional customs, at the expense of non european immigrants.
Vaclav Havel: he signed Charter 77, which criticized the government for ignoring human provisions of the Helsinki Accords and called on communist leaders to respect civil and political liberties.
Karol Wojtyla: He was elected to become Pope John Paul II, where he preached love of Christ and country and the rights of man. It rallied Polish nationalism.
Lech Walesa: organized Solidarity.
Solidarity: it was a national union that pursued a self limiting revolution, which meant it was mostly moderate and did not challenge the communist rule directly. However, it did organize nationwide strikes, which was still a challenge towards the communist party.
Wojciech Jaruzelski: He was angry about the economic crisis that Solidarity was causing, so he proclaimed martial law and arrested Solidarity leaders.
Mikhail Gorbachev: he realized that sour relations with the West in the cold war was causing the Soviet Union to fall behind. He emphasized economic structuring, or perestroika. He also made the government and media more open, heavily de-stalinization and rebuilding the Soviet Union. He also democratized the Soviet Union, and challenged the Congress of People's Deputies, which was witnessed by many, leading to many soviet citizens learning open discussion, critical thinking, and representative government.
Tadeusz Mazowiecki: He became the new noncommunist Prime Minister of Poland, and cautiously introduced revolutionary changes to avoid challenging Communist Party and bringing the Soviet Union in to stop the changes.
János Kádár: Communist leader of Hungary, he permitted liberalization of the rigid economy after the uprising in exchange for political obedience, and still managed to hold free elections in government.
Nicolae Ceauşescu: Dictator of Romania, he was faced with mass protest and ordered his security forces to quell it, leading to a violent and bloody revolution, and ultimately led to Ceauescu being captured and a coalition government was established.
Boris Yeltsin: Gorbachev was challenged by the Russian Soviet Republic, and he was rivaled by Yeltsin, the RSR parliamentary leader. He declared Russia independence from the USSR, and when Gorbachev was captured in a coup by the Communist old guard, Yeltsin saved him and eventually Gorbachev resigned, ending the Soviet Union.

Important Treaties/Plans

Helsinki Accords: an agreement between US, Canada, SU, and all European states (besides Albania and Andorra) that European political frontiers could not be changed by force. It also accepted provisions of guaranteeing civil rights and political freedom of citizens, signing an end to the Cold War.
Second Vatican Council: Catholic leaders agreed on a number of reforms meant to democratize and renew the church and broaden its appeal. They called for new openness in Catholic theology and declared that masses would henceforth be said in local languages rather than in Latin, which few could understand. These resolutions did little to halt the slide toward secularization, however.
Civil Rights Act of 1964: prohibited discrimination in public services and on the job.
Voting Rights Act of 1965: granted all african americans the right to vote
Brezhnev Doctrine: the Soviet Union and its allies had the right to intervene in any east bloc country to maintain communist rule.
Gdansk Agreement: government agreement to grant workers demands after massive strikes.
Paris Accord: a general peace treaty that brought an end to both World War II and Cold War.

Main Events

US Vietnam War: Vietnam won independence from France in 1954, but President Eisenhower refused to sign the Geneva Accords that temporarily divided COmmunist north and anti communist south. Eisenhower then sent troops to support south Vietnam to face insurgents. John F Kennedy increased the US military troops to 16k, and then President Johnson increased it to 500k. At the Tet offensive, the south attacked the north, and it was a major event of the war, signaling that it was not close to ending. Eventually President Nixon gradually disengaged America from Vietnam.
Student Revolts/May Events: students in France inspired by New Left ideals began protesting in University of Paris, and led to violent clashes with the police. The students caused so much chaos that it threatened the Fifth French Republic of Charles de Gaulle to collapse. He fixed the problem by improving working reforms and then held new elections, where his conservative party won by a lot.
OPEC incident: Basically it collapsed and led to economic crisis, and also it declared an embargo on the US because the US helped Israel in the war against Syria and Egypt.
Velvet Revolution: led by students and Vacla Havel, protesters took control of the streets and caused the Communist government to resign, and Czech elected Havel as their president.
German Unification: Three main causes: Opening of the Berlin wall, Helmut Kohl presented a 10 point unification plan and an exchange of East German marks for much more valuable West German marks. His conservative Alliance of Germany was supported by West German Chirsitian Democrats. The final cause was the reestablishment of a united and strong German state that would threaten the balance of power, so Kohl agreed to not develop nuclear, biological, or chemical weapons.
